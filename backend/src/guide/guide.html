Treat your agent like a new employee

The single most important thing to understand

The best way to think about your Otto agent is as a new hire on their first day. They're smart, capable, and ready to work — but they know nothing about your business yet. The quality of their work depends entirely on how well you onboard them.

Just like a real employee, your agent needs to understand:

1
Who they are
Their name, role, tone of voice, and personality.
2
What they do
Their responsibilities, tasks, and what success looks like.
3
Who they work for
Your company, your values, your customers, and how you operate.
4
Who they report to
You! And potentially other agents in the team.
💡 Pro tip
The more specific and detailed your setup, the better your agent performs. An agent with a vague description will give vague answers. An agent with a rich backstory, clear rules, and detailed knowledge will feel like magic.

Setting up your agent's personality

The files that define who your agent is

Your agent has two layers of knowledge: permanent configuration files that define its core behavior, and conversation memory that it builds up as you chat.

Layer 1: Configuration files (permanent — set during setup)

These files live on your agent's dedicated server and are loaded every time it starts. They define the agent's baseline personality and knowledge before any conversation happens.

✨ Personality
The core personality definition. Defines the agent's name, tone, communication style, values, and behavioral guidelines. This is who they are.
🪪 Profile
Detailed identity information: their role title, area of expertise, how they introduce themselves, and what they're specifically good at.
📋 Workspace Rules
Defines the team structure when multiple agents are involved. Who reports to whom, how they collaborate, and escalation paths.
🧠 Knowledge Base
Long-term memory and reference knowledge: your company details, products, services, processes, and anything the agent should always know.
📝 How to edit these
Go to Settings in your dashboard — click the ⚙️ icon on any agent card. You can edit all configuration directly from there.

Layer 2: Conversation memory (builds up as you chat)

As you talk to your agent, it remembers the conversation and builds context over time. You can tell it things like "My company sells organic coffee to restaurants in Lyon" and it will remember this for the rest of the session.

💡 What's the difference?
Think of configuration as the employee handbook your agent reads on day one — always there, always loaded. Conversation memory is like the notes they take during meetings — it persists across messages but may not survive a server restart. For critical information that must always be available, put it in the configuration.

Here's an example of what a good Personality setup looks like:

# Agent Personality Name: Eric Role: CFO & Financial Advisor Company: Bright Studios (creative agency) ## Personality You are precise, analytical, and calm. You communicate financial information clearly without unnecessary jargon. You're direct but never condescending. When you don't know something, you say so. ## Rules - Always present numbers with context (% change, comparison) - Flag expenses over €5,000 for human review - Never authorize payments — only recommend - When asked about legal matters, defer to the legal team - Keep a professional, warm tone in all communications ## Tasks - Monthly expense tracking and reporting - Budget forecasting and analysis - Invoice review and categorization - Financial Q&A for the team

Giving your agent the right context

What your agent needs to know about you

For your agent to serve you well, it needs to understand your world. The more relevant context you provide, the more useful and accurate the agent's responses will be.

🏢
Your company
What does your business do? Who are your customers? What's your industry? What makes you different?
👤
Your role
What's your position? What decisions do you make? What do you need help with?
📋
Your processes
How do you handle invoices? What's your approval workflow? What tools do you use?
🎯
Your preferences
How formal or casual should the agent be? What language? Should it ask for confirmation before acting?
💡 Example prompt to give your agent context
"I run a 15-person marketing agency in Paris. Our main clients are B2B SaaS companies. We use Notion for project management and Pennylane for accounting. I'm the CEO and I want you to help me track monthly expenses and flag anything unusual."

Information security

What to share with your agent — and what to hold back

Just like with a new employee, you wouldn't hand over the keys to everything on day one. Start with the information your agent needs for their immediate tasks, and expand access as you build trust and confidence.

⚠️ Important
Your agent runs on a dedicated, isolated server that only you can access. Your data is not shared with other customers. However, conversations are processed by AI models (like Claude or GPT) to generate responses. Never share passwords, API keys, credit card numbers, or highly sensitive credentials directly in chat.

Safe to share:

✅
Company information, processes, org structure, product details
✅
General financial figures, budgets, forecasts
✅
Customer names and general project context
✅
Documents, reports, and presentations

Be cautious with:

⚠️
Bank account details, tax IDs, social security numbers
⚠️
Passwords, API keys, private encryption keys
⚠️
Confidential legal documents before review
⚠️
Personal health or sensitive employee data

Understanding AI tokens

Why some tasks cost more than others

Every time your agent reads your message and generates a response, it uses AI tokens. Tokens are the currency of AI models — roughly, 1 token ≈ ¾ of a word. Both your input and the agent's output consume tokens.

Not all tasks are equal. A simple "What time is our meeting?" uses a handful of tokens. But asking your agent to analyze a 50-page financial report and write a summary could use 100× more.

⚠️ Token usage can add up quickly
Complex tasks — long documents, detailed analysis, code generation, multi-step reasoning — burn through tokens fast. If you're cost-sensitive, be mindful of how many complex requests you make, especially with premium models.

What affects token usage:

📏
Message length
Longer messages = more tokens in. Longer responses = more tokens out.
🧠
Task complexity
Analysis, reasoning, and creative tasks require the model to "think" more, which uses more tokens.
📎
Attachments & context
Sending documents, images, or referencing long conversation history adds tokens.
🤖
Model choice
Smarter models (like Claude Opus) cost more per token than lighter models (like Haiku).

Choosing the right AI model

Match the model to the task to save money

Otto gives each agent access to 300+ AI models through OpenRouter. The default is Claude Sonnet 4.5, which offers an excellent balance of intelligence and cost. But you can switch models to optimize for either cost or performance.

💡 The golden rule
Use cheap models for simple tasks, expensive models for complex ones. There's no reason to use a $75/M-token model to answer "What's the weather today?"
ModelBest forCostTier
Claude Haiku 4.5 Quick answers, classification, simple tasks $0.80 / $4 per 1M Budget
Gemini 3 Flash Fast responses, basic analysis, high volume Free – $0.30 / $1.25 Free/Cheap
Claude Sonnet 4.5 General purpose, writing, analysis, coding $3 / $15 per 1M Standard ★
GPT-4o Creative writing, nuanced reasoning $2.50 / $10 per 1M Standard
Claude Opus 4.6 Complex reasoning, research, expert-level tasks $15 / $75 per 1M Premium

Example setups:

💰
Customer support agent (budget-focused)
Use Claude Haiku 4.5 or Gemini Flash — fast, cheap, handles FAQs and routing well.
⚖️
General business assistant (balanced)
Use Claude Sonnet 4.5 (default) — great all-rounder for email drafts, analysis, and planning.
🔬
CFO / Strategic analyst (accuracy-focused)
Use Claude Opus 4.6 — when you need deep reasoning, complex financial analysis, or expert-level output.
